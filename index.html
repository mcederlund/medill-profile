Martinez's Legacy

Martinez's care for children, the environment and her community have turned Humboldt Park into the neighborhood it is today. Martinez, with the help of then-Chicago Mayor Richard Daley and local organizers, successfully campaigned for the Humboldt Park Library's construction.

She's advocated for affordable housing by working as a Bickerdike Redevelopment Corporation board member and for the Neighborhood Housing Service.

She's also organized an annual Earth Day cleanup (one cleanup drew 400 people), and every day during the pandemic, Martinez and her husband, Mario, would walk through Humboldt Park and pick up litter. Martinez is also the founder and president of the now-closed Block Club Federation and was a facilitator for 33 years with the Chicago Police Department's 14th District.

And after her 16-year-old son was fatally shot in the head at a high school graduation party, Martinez became a teacher's assistant at her church ‚Äì‚Äì Maternity at the Blessed Virgin Mary Catholic Church ‚Äì‚Äì to distract herself from the grief. Character and Values

Martinez's advocacy, morals and sense of leadership are unapologetic and unrelenting. She's fearless, too. When she saw teenagers, sometimes former students of hers, dealing drugs on her street, she approached them and asked them to stop and go somewhere else. They'd always listen, she said.

People familiar with Martinez and her work described her as honest, helpful, inspirational, upfront and dedicated. She's well-known, well-respected and well-loved in the community she helped build.

Her voice is anything but quiet.

Formative Influences

Martinez credits four things that have turned her into the leader she is today: her mother, the Humboldt Park-based Youth Service Project, a leadership program at DePaul University and God.

Martinez's mother raised three children by herself and took up work at a factory where she learned how to "punch press." (Martinez and I both admitted we didn't know what that meant.) Her father left seven days after the birth of her youngest brother, Alex.

Community Voices

Former Humboldt Park Alderman Billy Ocasio has worked extensively with Martinez to better the neighborhood. "We've done a lot of things together," he said. "We've worked on school issues together. We've worked on green space issues together, on neighborhoods, on housing and business."

Ocasio, now president and CEO of the National Museum of Puerto Rican Arts and Culture, has become great friends with Martinez. "We've just done so much together," Ocasio said with a laugh. "I can't recall everything."

Martinez has a soft spot for children, who she said are "always in my heart." Feliz Palacios-Villafane reflected on how Martinez encouraged Humboldt Park's youth to pursue their dream careers.

She encouraged a lot, and I learned from her, and I did the same thing. I encourage the block.

Martinez also encouraged Carlos Rivas, a Chicago Board of Education board member, to run for his position, Palacios-Villafane said.

Martinez's way of bringing people together has been pivotal in his personal and professional development, Rivas said. "That's really shaped who I am and shaped what I do, and why I went into service and teaching and now elected leadership was because of her example and her pushing me," he said.

Rivas met Martinez at a back-to-school event when he was a boy, and the two have stayed good friends since. "I saw in him the leadership that he had, and he was only 10 years old," Martinez said.

Martinez treats Rivas like a son and invites him to her weekly Wednesday family dinners, he said. When his mother was in hospice and later passed, Martinez showed up for Rivas "when I needed the most."

Martinez is a community organizer at heart, Rivas said.
